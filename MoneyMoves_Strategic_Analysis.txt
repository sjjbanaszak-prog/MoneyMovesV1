SECTION-BY-SECTION SWOT
================================================================================


PENSION
-------

Strengths
- HMRC carry-forward calculations (FIFO, 3-year lookback) are genuinely rare in
  consumer apps — most tools only show current-year usage
- Salary sacrifice vs personal contribution comparison with real 20%/40%/45% tax
  relief is more accurate than most calculators
- IRR per provider from uploaded statement data gives actual performance
  benchmarking, not just balance growth
- Multi-provider tracking with intelligent CSV/Excel column mapping lowers the
  data entry barrier
- Peer comparison creates emotional engagement

Weaknesses
- No State Pension integration — a significant gap given the State Pension is
  most users' largest single retirement asset
- No defined benefit / final salary pension support — a large portion of the UK
  workforce is excluded
- CSV upload only; no connection to PensionDashboard.gov.uk or the Pension
  Finder Service
- Peer comparison data is static/synthetic, which users will eventually see through

Opportunities
- Pension Finder Service / MaPS API — when it goes live, early integration is a
  genuine differentiator
- State Pension forecast input (NI years + expected retirement date) — high
  value, medium effort
- Defined benefit modelling (accrual rate, normal pension age, commutation) —
  opens up a large user segment

Threats
- PensionBee, Nutmeg, and Moneybox offer managed pension products with built-in
  dashboards — lower friction than upload-based tracking
- DWP "Check Your State Pension" is free and authoritative
- The pension dashboard ecosystem (when mature) may make standalone trackers
  redundant


MORTGAGE
--------

Strengths
- The health score across 5 factors (LTV, payment consistency, rate security,
  capital progress, property appreciation) is genuinely differentiated — no other
  UK consumer app does this
- Real Land Registry UKHPI data avoids the Zoopla/Rightmove credibility problem
- Overpayment scenario modelling (regular + one-off, multiple frequencies) is
  more capable than most bank calculators
- Rate change modelling (initial fixed → SVR) correctly reflects how UK
  mortgages actually work
- Deal switch calculator shows real cost/benefit of switching vs staying

Weaknesses
- No live mortgage rate data — rates are manually entered, so "market
  comparison" is impossible
- No affordability calculator (what can I borrow?) — a top-of-funnel acquisition
  tool that's entirely absent
- Single mortgage only — portfolio landlords and let-to-buy setups are excluded
- No remortgage reminder when the fixed term is approaching expiry — the most
  valuable alert possible

Opportunities
- Live rate feeds via Moneyfacts or a broker API — transforms the section from
  calculator to advisor
- Remortgage expiry alert (push notification when within 6 months of reverting
  to SVR)
- Stamp duty / SDLT calculator (first-time buyer, second home, buy-to-let
  scenarios)
- Affordability calculator linked to the Income section

Threats
- Habito, Trussle, L&C Mortgages offer free comparisons plus broker service —
  they monetise via lender referrals and can undercut on price
- MortgageGym and Dashly do live rate tracking
- Bank apps (HSBC, Nationwide) now show real-time mortgage balances and
  remaining terms


SAVINGS
-------

Strengths
- ISA allowance tracking from real transaction data (not user-entered figures) is
  more accurate than any competitor
- Premium Bonds analysis is genuinely unique — no other UK consumer fintech
  models this properly
- LISA allowance tracking with government bonus calculation is niche but highly
  valued by the under-40 segment
- Intelligent column mapping for varied CSV formats reduces upload friction
  significantly
- Monthly balance change visualisation surfaces patterns users wouldn't otherwise
  notice

Weaknesses
- CSV upload only — no Open Banking means manual re-upload every time data needs
  refreshing
- No savings goal setting (target amount, target date, monthly required
  contribution) — the most obvious missing feature
- No interest rate comparison or best-buy alerts — users can see their data but
  can't act on it
- No emergency fund benchmark ("your 3-month buffer target is £X, you're at Y%")

Opportunities
- Open Banking via TrueLayer or Plaid UK — transforms the section from periodic
  snapshot to live dashboard
- Savings goal tracking with progress bars and projected achievement dates —
  medium effort, high engagement
- Best-buy savings rate integration (Moneyfacts API) — "your Easy Access ISA
  pays 1.8%, you could get 5.1%"
- NS&I direct API for Premium Bonds balance — removes the need to upload for the
  most unique feature

Threats
- Emma, Plum, Chip, and MoneyDashboard all have Open Banking and are free —
  the upload workflow is a meaningful friction disadvantage
- Monzo and Starling now show ISA contribution tracking natively within their apps


INCOME
------

Strengths
- Accurate UK 2025/26 tax and NI calculations including the correct thresholds
- Take-home comparison view allows basic scenario modelling

Weaknesses
- This is the thinnest section in the app by a considerable margin — it is
  essentially a take-home calculator with a nav item
- No payslip import or verification — all figures are manually entered
- No support for self-employment, freelance, dividends, or multiple income
  sources
- No salary benchmarking by role or region — the most valuable addition for
  most users
- Overlaps with the Pension Builder's income inputs, creating confusion about
  where the "source of truth" lives

Opportunities
- Payslip PDF/CSV upload and parsing — would make this section genuinely useful
  vs a web calculator
- Salary benchmarking by job title and location (ONS ASHE data is publicly
  available)
- IR35 and inside/outside determination for contractors — a significant
  underserved audience
- Tax code checker and explanation — a meaningful number of UK workers are on
  the wrong tax code

Threats
- HMRC's own tax calculator is free and authoritative
- MoneySavingExpert's take-home calculator is trusted and well-known
- Without significant investment, the Income section risks being perceived as a
  feature rather than a section — it may not justify its nav real estate


INVESTMENTS
-----------

Strengths
- Trading 212 CSV parsing with treemap, monthly performance and IRR is more
  capable than Trading 212's own analytics
- Asset allocation treemap gives a visual breakdown most platforms don't offer
- Monthly performance tracking is polished

Weaknesses
- Only supports Trading 212 — excludes users of Hargreaves Lansdown, AJ Bell,
  Vanguard, Freetrade, Interactive Investor, Nutmeg, and every other UK platform.
  This is the majority of UK retail investors
- No real-time prices — all data is static from the last upload; portfolio value
  is always stale
- No benchmark comparison (vs FTSE 100, S&P 500, global index) — users cannot
  assess their performance meaningfully
- No capital gains tax calculation — critical since the CGT allowance dropped to
  £3k in 2024/25
- ISA vs non-ISA holdings are not differentiated — the tax implications are
  entirely ignored

Opportunities
- Multi-broker CSV support (HL Flex, Vanguard, Freetrade all export parseable
  CSVs)
- CGT calculator and annual reporting — high value given the current £3k
  allowance
- Dividend income tracking and forecasting — popular with income investors
- Real-time portfolio valuation via Yahoo Finance or OpenFIGI — free APIs
  available

Threats
- Sharesight directly competes on multi-broker portfolio tracking and does it
  comprehensively
- HL, Vanguard, and AJ Bell are improving their own analytics dashboards
- The Trading 212-only constraint means the section is invisible to the majority
  of potential users


FAMILY
------

Strengths
- Combined household allowances (ISA + pension + JISA + Junior SIPP + Premium
  Bonds) is genuinely unique in UK consumer fintech
- Live sync from primary user's Savings and Pension sections means no data
  re-entry — a UX detail competitors miss
- Children's accounts at the correct JISA/Junior SIPP/Premium Bonds limits is
  technically accurate
- HICBC warning on Child Benefit is practically valuable and rarely surfaced
  elsewhere
- The read-only design for primary user data ("manage it from your own section")
  is the right call

Weaknesses
- Partner data is entirely manually entered — there is no way for a partner to
  create a linked account and share real data
- No inheritance or estate planning angle — the natural extension of a family
  finance section
- The mixture of live-synced and manually-entered figures could confuse users
  about what's current

Opportunities
- Partner account linking (invite by email, accept, share selected financial
  data) — would make the Family section dramatically more powerful and justify
  the premium plan price
- Children's savings projection to age 18/21/25 — emotionally compelling, drives
  engagement and upgrades
- Inheritance / estate tax calculator — high net worth families would find
  significant value

Threats
- Family finance management is relatively niche; most households manage finances
  separately
- The manual partner data entry undermines trust in the combined view until
  account linking exists


DEBT
----

The Debt Manager is DISABLED — the nav item exists at 35% opacity but the
section does not. This is the most glaring gap in the product. UK household debt
(credit cards, personal loans, car finance, student loans) represents the primary
source of financial stress for the target demographic. Avalanche vs snowball
repayment comparison is a well-validated retention driver. Emma, Snoop, and
MoneyDashboard all include it. Users with debt — likely a significant proportion
of the user base — have nothing here.

PLATFORM SWOT
================================================================================

Strengths
- Deep UK specificity is a genuine moat — HMRC rules, tax year, ISA/LISA/JISA
  limits, UK mortgage structures. This is not a generic app with a £ sign bolted on
- Cross-section integration (savings and pension feeding the family section, net
  worth aggregation) shows architectural maturity rare at this stage
- The Family section is a meaningful differentiator — no UK consumer app combines
  adult and children's allowances with this depth
- Demo mode with realistic data lowers the sign-up barrier significantly
- Mobile-first design with consistent visual language is polished and coherent
- Pricing (Individual £5.99/mo, Family £7.99/mo) is sensible for the value
  proposition

Weaknesses
- No Open Banking — the most significant structural weakness. Every major
  competitor has it. Upload-only means data is always stale, friction is high,
  and retention is harder
- The Debt section doesn't exist, and Income and Investments are underpowered
  to the point of being liabilities
- AI features are largely aspirational — multiple "See X Insights" buttons exist
  but personalisation is thin
- No proactive alerts or push notifications — the app is entirely reactive; users
  must remember to return to it
- No budgeting or spending categorisation — the most common entry point for
  personal finance apps is entirely absent

Opportunities
- Open Banking would be the single highest-ROI investment — it makes every
  section better simultaneously
- A budgeting / spending tracker massively expands the addressable audience and
  daily active use
- Proactive alerts (ISA allowance running low, fixed rate expiring, salary
  received) drive retention
- Partner account linking strengthens the Family plan's value proposition
  substantially
- Regulated advice or robo-advice integration unlocks premium monetisation at a
  higher price point

Threats
- Emma, Plum, MoneyDashboard, and Snoop all have Open Banking, are free or
  freemium, and have established user bases
- Monzo and Starling are building financial management features into their
  banking apps — they already own the transaction data
- MaPS (Money and Pensions Service) provides free, government-backed tools
  including pension guidance
- FCA COBS rules around financial promotions and advice boundaries require
  careful navigation as AI features mature

MISSING FUNCTIONALITY — PRIORITISED BUILD LIST
================================================================================

#1  Open Banking integration                                    Effort: High | MUST-HAVE
    Connect bank accounts via TrueLayer/Plaid UK; auto-refresh savings, current
    accounts, and debt balances. Removes all upload friction — data is always
    live. Makes every other section better simultaneously.

#2  Debt Manager                                                Effort: High | MUST-HAVE
    Avalanche vs snowball repayment modelling, credit card interest calculations,
    debt-free date projections. The nav item already exists — the section needs
    building.

#3  Savings Goals                                               Effort: Low  | MUST-HAVE
    Set target amount + target date per account; track progress; calculate
    required monthly contribution. Drives regular app opens and emotional
    engagement.

#4  Proactive Alerts / Push Notifications                       Effort: Medium | MUST-HAVE
    ISA allowance running low; fixed mortgage rate expiring in 90 days; annual
    pension allowance approaching; salary received. The primary driver of
    retention in any finance app.

#5  Budget & Spending Tracker                                   Effort: High | High
    Categorise transactions (via Open Banking); monthly budget vs actual;
    spending trends. The most common entry point for personal finance apps —
    entirely absent today.

#6  Income Section Rebuild                                      Effort: Medium | High
    Payslip import, salary benchmarking by role/region (ONS ASHE data),
    self-employment support, IR35 calculator, tax code checker. Current section
    is a calculator pretending to be a module.

#7  Multi-Broker Investment Support                             Effort: Medium | High
    Parse CSVs from HL, AJ Bell, Vanguard, Freetrade, Interactive Investor
    alongside Trading 212. Opens the investments section to the majority of UK
    retail investors.

#8  Capital Gains Tax Calculator                                Effort: Medium | High
    Track unrealised and realised gains across taxable investment accounts;
    project CGT liability. CGT allowance is now £3k — every non-ISA investor
    needs this.

#9  Partner Account Linking                                     Effort: High | High
    Invite partner by email; they create an account; selected financial data
    shared to Family section. Makes the Family plan's value proposition
    substantially stronger.

#10 Remortgage Expiry Alert                                     Effort: Low  | High
    Alert when fixed rate is within 90/180 days of expiring; link to rate
    comparison. The highest-value mortgage alert possible — most people miss
    their window.

#11 State Pension Tracker                                       Effort: Low  | High
    Input NI years, forecast remaining years; estimate State Pension income
    alongside private pension projection. State Pension is most users' largest
    retirement asset.

#12 Live Mortgage Rate Comparison                               Effort: Medium | Medium
    Integrate a rate feed (Moneyfacts) to show current best-buy rates vs user's
    existing deal. Transforms the mortgage section from calculator to advisor;
    natural funnel to broker referral revenue.

#13 Savings Rate Comparison                                     Effort: Low  | Medium
    Show best-buy rates for each account type vs user's current rate.
    "You could earn £X more per year by switching."

#14 Children's Savings Projections                              Effort: Low  | Medium
    Project child's JISA/SIPP/Premium Bonds to age 18, 21, 57 at different
    contribution rates. Emotionally compelling; drives Family plan adoption.

#15 IR35 / Contractor Calculator                                Effort: Medium | Medium
    Inside vs outside IR35; limited company vs umbrella vs PAYE take-home
    comparison. Large underserved contractor audience with high disposable income.


================================================================================
KEY OBSERVATION
================================================================================

The gap between what Open Banking would unlock and the current upload-only model
is so large it should be treated as an architectural decision, not a feature.
Everything else on this list becomes easier and more valuable once transaction
data is live. Building features on top of stale, upload-based data is building
on sand.

The three things that would move the needle most in the short term — and don't
require Open Banking — are:

  1. Debt Manager    — the nav item already exists, it just needs building
  2. Savings Goals   — low effort, high retention impact
  3. Remortgage alert — a single notification that saves users real money
                        and builds trust
